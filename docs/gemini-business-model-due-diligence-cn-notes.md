## Purpose

This document captures incremental lessons from the Gemini report on value-investor business-model diligence. It is tracked separately from the earlier user-provided playbook because it emphasizes a more fieldwork-heavy, scuttlebutt-heavy research style.

## Main Incremental Ideas

The report frames business-model understanding as the search for durable excess returns:

- the business model is the mechanism that generates a spread between ROIC and cost of capital,
- the moat protects that spread from competition,
- the investor's task is to test whether the apparent moat exists in the real world.

## Industry And Company Analysis

The report emphasizes a two-layer framework:

1. Industry structure: profit pool, competitive intensity, entry barriers, consolidation, substitution, technology disruption, vertical or horizontal integration risk.
2. Company-specific position: how the company creates differentiated value through product, cost, intangible assets, distribution, operating skill, or management execution.

This is useful for the Business Model / Moat Agent because it prevents the agent from analyzing a company in isolation.

## Scale Economies Shared

The report highlights `scale economies shared` as a major moat pattern:

- scale lowers unit cost,
- management shares the cost advantage with customers through lower prices or better value,
- lower prices drive more volume,
- more volume improves supplier power and asset utilization,
- the cycle repeats.

This pattern is especially relevant for businesses like Costco, Amazon, PDD/Temu, and other low-price marketplace or logistics models. For PDD, it should be treated as a hypothesis to test, not a conclusion.

## Scuttlebutt Research

The report ranks scuttlebutt as high-ROI qualitative research once desk work has identified the key questions.

Important source groups:

- customers: willingness to pay, switching costs, repeat behavior, product quality,
- suppliers/distributors/dealers: bargaining power, payment behavior, inventory movement, channel health,
- competitors: grudging respect can be strong evidence of a real moat,
- former employees: culture, internal KPIs, bottlenecks, and gap between external narrative and internal reality.

The system should treat these as source lines for external moat validation and customer happiness work.

## Expert Networks And Channel Checks

The report separates:

- channel checks: broader, repeatable monitoring of distribution, inventory, pricing, and product flow,
- expert networks: expensive but useful for narrow technical or operational questions.

Both require compliance controls and should be used only after the research question is specific.

## Fieldwork And Industry Clusters

The report strongly emphasizes physical investigation:

- store visits,
- factory visits,
- logistics node visits,
- industry-cluster investigation,
- product teardown,
- channel observation,
- forensic checks of whether reported operations match physical reality.

This is highest ROI when the target business is tangible, operationally complex, fraud-prone, supply-chain-dependent, or when a core valuation assumption depends on physical execution.

## Compliance And Mosaic Theory

The report warns that primary research, expert calls, and alternative data can create MNPI risk.

Any future agent that uses interviews, channel checks, or expert networks should record:

- source type,
- whether the source could have access to confidential information,
- question boundaries,
- call notes or source notes,
- compliance review status,
- whether the evidence is public, non-material, or needs human review.

## Research Sequencing

The report's suggested sequence:

1. Complete secondary research and model the apparent moat.
2. Identify the knowledge gaps that cannot be answered from documents.
3. Deploy expert networks and channel checks for targeted questions.
4. Send senior analysts into fieldwork with specific hypotheses.
5. Synthesize the evidence as a mosaic.

This is compatible with our system if we keep fieldwork and interviews as hypothesis-driven escalation rather than broad data gathering.

## System Design Implications

The Gemini report suggests these future improvements:

- add an industry structure mapper,
- add a scale-economies-shared hypothesis template,
- add scuttlebutt source lines for customers, suppliers, competitors, and former employees,
- add fieldwork investigation planning,
- add channel-check and expert-network evidence schemas,
- add MNPI/compliance flags for primary research evidence,
- add anti-fraud / physical-reality checks for companies where reported operations can be inspected.
