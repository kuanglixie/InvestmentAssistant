## Purpose

This document captures the key lessons from the user-provided business-model research playbook so they can be tracked as learning material for the multi-agent stock research system.

The playbook's central idea is that understanding a business model means converting narrative into an economic engine:

- who pays,
- why they pay,
- what it costs to acquire and serve them,
- why they stay,
- how much capital the model consumes,
- how much owner cash can be generated through a cycle,
- what can break the model before the market notices.

## Core Investor Questions

The Business Model / Moat Agent should eventually answer five core questions:

1. What is the customer value proposition?
2. What are the unit economics?
3. What protects returns from competition?
4. How does management allocate capital?
5. What can break the model quickly enough to impair intrinsic value?

These questions map directly to the user's investing principle:

- right business model,
- right people,
- right price.

## Decision Criteria

The playbook turns business-model research into decision criteria rather than loose topics.

| Criterion | What Must Be Tested | Evidence Needed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business-model clarity | Who pays, for what, through which channel, at what margin, with what capital needs | Filing-based revenue map, cost map, customer/value-chain map |
| Economic viability | Unit economics are positive or clearly improving with scale | Contribution economics by product, customer, geography, or segment |
| Durability | Returns are protected by switching costs, cost advantage, network density, regulation, brand, data, or scale | Peer comparison, customer/supplier evidence, retention/pricing evidence, moat tests |
| Capital efficiency | Growth does not consume disproportionate capital | ROIC, incremental ROIC, maintenance/growth capex view, working-capital analysis |
| Governance quality | Incentives align with per-share value creation | Proxy, ownership, compensation metrics, buyback/M&A record |
| Accounting trustworthiness | Reported economics approximate underlying economics | Footnotes, CAMs, non-GAAP bridge, restatement history, stable KPI definitions |
| Valuation sufficiency | Margin of safety compensates for residual uncertainty | Scenario model, reverse DCF, downside appraisal |

## ROI-Ranked Research Sequence

The playbook argues for staged escalation:

1. Filing stack and accounting deep dive.
2. Rebuild unit economics and driver tree.
3. Industry structure and competitor/value-chain mapping.
4. Scenario model, reverse DCF, and downside case.
5. Proxy, governance, and capital-allocation review.
6. Product, pricing, and customer-journey testing.
7. Targeted customer/supplier/distributor/former-employee interviews.
8. Site visits, mystery shopping, and channel checks.
9. Alternative data.
10. Management and IR follow-up.
11. Specialist expert-network calls.
12. Broad surveys only when narrowly targeted.

The system should not maximize research activity for its own sake. It should escalate only when the remaining unknown could change valuation or conviction.

## Filing-First Rule

The highest-ROI source stack starts with official materials:

- 10-K / 20-F: business description, segments, revenue recognition, risks, accounting notes.
- 10-Q / interim reports: KPI progression, seasonality, one-time items.
- 8-K / material announcements: material events, management changes, restatements, auditor issues.
- DEF 14A / proxy: incentives, ownership, governance, related parties.
- Debt documents: maturities, covenants, financing risk.
- IR presentations and transcripts: useful framing, but lower priority than filings.
- Company website, pricing pages, app stores, terms: real product and pricing evidence.
- Competitor filings and websites: alternative economics and moat boundaries.
- Official industry/macro data: cost, demand, inflation, and market-size anchors.

## Evidence Rules

Every major claim should have:

- source link or local path,
- evidence type,
- confidence score,
- source quality label,
- failure mode,
- valuation relevance.

The research process should distinguish:

- fact,
- calculation,
- interpretation,
- hypothesis,
- unresolved issue,
- human-review gate.

## Primary Research Rules

Primary research should be hypothesis-led, not broad curiosity gathering.

Customer, supplier, distributor, former-employee, competitor, and specialist interviews should focus on behavior and economics:

- why customers buy,
- what alternatives they considered,
- what would make them switch,
- who has bargaining power,
- where bottlenecks appear,
- what is hard or easy to copy.

Social/forum/review evidence remains low-confidence lead evidence unless triangulated.

## Product And Fieldwork Rules

Product testing should capture:

- search and purchase journey,
- actual price and fees,
- bundling or upsell logic,
- delivery/use experience,
- support and return/cancel friction,
- competitor comparison for the same use case.

Site visits and fieldwork should be used after the agent knows what to test. They are especially useful for tangible, operationally complex, route-density, logistics, retail, local-network, or turnaround businesses.

## Scenario And Valuation Rules

The playbook treats margin of safety as the end of the process, not a substitute for research.

Valuation should include:

- revenue driver tree,
- cost tree,
- capital tree,
- base/bear/bull cases,
- reverse DCF,
- kill criteria,
- margin of safety tied to uncertainty.

The required margin of safety should widen when uncertainty about unit economics, capital intensity, or moat durability is high.
